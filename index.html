We decompose climate variation into five components, estimate how each affects yields, and translate the results into decision-ready financial intelligence.

Local Heat Shocks: The Invisible Damage

Idiosyncratic temperature shocks — unpredictable local events like heat waves and unseasonal frost — cause the most yield damage and are hardest to insure against.

Yield Impact per 1σ Temperature Shock — with 90% Confidence Intervals

Point estimate and 90% CI for each crop. Grey = not statistically significant (CI crosses zero).

Regression Result

A 1σ local temperature shock reduces Kharif yields by 6.6% on average. Pulses are hit hardest: Moong -18.9%, Arhar/Tur -15.9%. Rice is nearly immune (-1.9%, not significant).

Identification Check

The temperature trend coefficient flips from +0.049 (confounded) to −0.064 (true warming damage) after controlling for technology adoption via year trends. Covariate and idiosyncratic shock coefficients are unchanged across specifications — confirming clean causal identification of shock effects.

GCM vs Trend Extrapolation

The 35-model CMIP6 ensemble median projects +0.55°C national average Kharif warming by 2035 under SSP5-8.5, versus +0.19°C from simple trend extrapolation. The difference reflects committed warming from ocean heat lag, accelerating radiative forcing, and climate feedbacks that linear extrapolation of 1997–2020 observations cannot capture. 100% of districts face negative Kharif yield impacts under both scenarios.

Spotlight

Parbhani, Maharashtra: +0.44°C by 2035 under SSP5-8.5 → -4.2% Kharif yield decline. With only 12% irrigation, this district has almost no climate buffer. Under SSP2-4.5 (moderate pathway): +0.40°C, -3.8%. Northern mountain districts face the steepest warming (Leh: +1.22°C, -11.2% yield).

09

Methodology

Following Burney, McIntosh et al. (PNAS 2024), we decompose district-level climate variation into five interpretable components, then estimate heterogeneous yield response functions using panel fixed effects with entity-clustered standard errors.

ComponentIdentificationPolicy Match
Baseline (μi)District mean across all yearsCrop choice (already adapted)
Trend (τit)District-specific OLS on yearLong-term infrastructure
Seasonal (sim)Detrended monthly meansPlanting calendar
Covariate (ωt)National mean of residualsParametric insurance
Idiosyncratic (εit)Remaining local variationIndex/indemnity insurance
DiCRA LayersNDVI, Soil Moisture, LULC, LST, SOCSpatial downscaling & cross-validation

Data

DiCRA geospatial backbone (NDVI, LULC, soil, LST), ERA5-Land 0.1° reanalysis (Copernicus), crop APY from data.gov.in, district shapefiles from Census 2011, irrigation from Land Use Statistics 2023-24.

Panel

673 districts × 12 crops × 24 years = ~126,000 observations across 35 states and union territories.

Estimation

PanelOLS with district fixed effects, entity-clustered standard errors. Interactions: climate components × irrigation share (time-invariant).

Theory

Two-period credit-adaptation model: decomposition provides sufficient statistics for optimal instrument allocation across adaptation investment (trend), parametric insurance (covariate), and credit risk adjustment (idiosyncratic). Full framework →

Forecast Skill

Leave-one-state-out cross-validation: average out-of-sample R² = 0.34. Temporal validation (train 1997–2015, predict 2016–2020): R² = 0.33. The model generalizes across both space and time.

Robustness

All climate variables remain statistically significant under Conley (1999) spatial HAC standard errors with a 200km kernel — SEs are 27–32% larger than entity-clustered but inference is unchanged.

What Kind of Climate Risk Does India Face?

Not all climate variation is equal. Our decomposition reveals what's predictable — and what isn't.

Temperature

98% predictable

Baseline climate and seasonal cycles dominate. Farmers and insurers can plan for this.

Precipitation

16% unpredictable local shocks

One in six units of rainfall variation is a local surprise — hailstorms, microbursts, localized flooding. This is what index insurance must cover.
